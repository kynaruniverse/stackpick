Quick Answer

Buy the Logitech G Pro X 2 if you want proprietary GX switches with a range of options, a clean no-frills design, and proven Logitech software reliability. Slightly more premium at a higher price.

Buy the HyperX Alloy Origins if you want HyperX's own linear switches, a full-aluminium frame at a lower price, and a TKL that's easier to find on sale in the UK.

Detailed Breakdown

The Big One: Wireless vs Wired

The G Pro X 2 LIGHTSPEED is wireless. The HyperX Alloy Origins is wired only. This is the most significant structural difference between the two. Logitech's LIGHTSPEED 2.4GHz wireless has zero perceptible latency difference from wired at gaming use ‚Äî it's the technology used in their pro-grade mice and is widely trusted. If a clean, cable-free desk is important to you, the G Pro X 2 wins on this point alone. If you're purely competitive and don't care about cables, the wired Alloy Origins is fine ‚Äî and significantly cheaper.

Build Quality: Both Are Excellent, for Different Reasons

The HyperX Alloy Origins has a full aircraft-grade aluminium body ‚Äî not just a top plate, the entire chassis. At under ¬£100 this is exceptional value. It feels like a ¬£150 keyboard. The G Pro X 2 uses an aluminium top plate with a slightly more complex internal construction. Both feel premium; the Alloy Origins arguably wins on raw build feel per pound spent.

Switches: GX vs HyperX Linear

GX Red switches are light linear switches with a 45g actuation force and smooth travel. They're widely liked and have no major complaints in community feedback. HyperX Red Linear switches are also light linears at 45g with a shorter 1.8mm actuation distance (vs GX Red's 2.0mm). The shorter actuation makes HyperX Reds feel slightly snappier for rapid keypresses. In blind testing, most people cannot reliably tell these apart ‚Äî but the HyperX actuation point is technically faster.

Software: G HUB vs NGENUITY

G HUB has had reliability issues historically but has improved substantially. NGENUITY (HyperX) is simpler and more stable but less feature-rich. Both do onboard memory profiles, which means your settings follow the keyboard to any PC without software installed. For competitive use, both are adequate; neither is a strong reason to choose one keyboard over the other.

USB Passthrough

The Alloy Origins includes a USB-A passthrough port ‚Äî you can plug a mouse or USB stick directly into the keyboard. The G Pro X 2 does not have this. If your PC is under a desk or otherwise inconvenient to reach, a keyboard USB passthrough is a quality-of-life feature that's easy to underestimate.

At ~¬£80‚Äì100, the HyperX Alloy Origins TKL represents extraordinary value: full aluminium, great switches, HyperX's solid reputation. At ~¬£160‚Äì180, the G Pro X 2 adds wireless ‚Äî but wireless alone doesn't justify ¬£60‚Äì70 extra for every buyer. If wireless matters to you, the G Pro X 2 is correctly priced for what it offers. If wired is fine, the Alloy Origins is objectively better value.

Our Verdict

The HyperX Alloy Origins TKL is the better value keyboard ‚Äî full aluminium, great switches, USB passthrough, and a price that leaves budget for other components. For pure competitive wired use, it's difficult to recommend spending ¬£60+ more on the G Pro X 2.

The G Pro X 2 is the right choice specifically if wireless is your priority. LIGHTSPEED is genuinely excellent and if you're cable-averse, the premium is justified. Outside of that use case, the Alloy Origins is the smarter buy.
